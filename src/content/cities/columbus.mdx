## How to Choose a Forensic Accountant in Columbus

- **Lead with credentials, not just CPA.** A general CPA is not a forensic accountant. Look specifically for CFF (Certified in Financial Forensics), CFE (Certified Fraud Examiner), or ABV/CVA if business valuation is in scope. These aren't honorary — they require demonstrated casework and continuing education.
- **Match the expert to your case type.** Columbus's market skews toward commercial litigation, insurance subrogation, and divorce-related financial analysis. Someone with deep embezzlement experience in healthcare (relevant given OhioHealth and Nationwide Children's) is a different animal than a business interruption specialist.
- **Ask for prior testimony history in Ohio state court.** Franklin County judges have seen enough expert witnesses to be skeptical of overly polished reports that can't withstand cross. You want someone who's been deposed in Ohio, not just someone who's written reports.
- **Check for conflicts early.** Columbus's professional accounting community is smaller than you'd expect for a city this size. If the opposing party retained a forensic accountant, there's a real chance your shortlist overlaps with theirs.
- **Get a scope agreement before engagement letters.** Forensic engagements have a way of expanding — a fraud investigation that starts with one business unit has a habit of growing legs. A clear written scope protects both sides.

> **Pro Tip:** If your case involves a Columbus-area franchise or distribution business, ask whether the expert has worked with Ohio's Uniform Commercial Code disputes specifically. The damages calculations in those cases have quirks that generalists routinely underestimate.

## What to Expect

Forensic accounting engagements in Columbus typically run $5,000–$75,000 depending on case complexity, document volume, and whether expert testimony is required — a fraud investigation involving years of records and a full trial appearance lands toward the top end, while a targeted damages rebuttal report can come in under $15,000. Most engagements follow a predictable arc: document review and transaction reconstruction (2–6 weeks), draft report, expert review session with counsel, and then the final report or deposition prep phase.

> **Reality Check:** The most common pricing mistake attorneys make is hiring on hourly rate alone. A $350/hour forensic accountant who needs 200 hours to produce a report that gets shredded on cross is more expensive than a $500/hour specialist who completes a tighter report in 80 hours and holds up under deposition. Credentials and testimony track record are better proxies for value than billing rate.

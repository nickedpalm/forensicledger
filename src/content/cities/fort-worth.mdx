## How to Choose a Forensic Accountant in Fort Worth

- **Verify credentials first, résumé second.** A CFF (Certified in Financial Forensics) or CFE (Certified Fraud Examiner) signals the examiner has passed rigorous competency standards — not just years in a tax practice. For business valuation disputes common in Tarrant County courts, look for CVA or ABV designations.
- **Match the expert to the matter type.** Someone who specializes in embezzlement and asset tracing is a different animal from one who does business interruption claims for insurers. Ask for a case list, not a general bio.
- **Demand prior testimony experience.** Texas courts are skeptical of experts who've never been cross-examined. If they've never testified in a Tarrant County or federal Northern District of Texas proceeding, that gap will show under pressure.
- **Check for conflicts early.** Fort Worth's business community is tightly networked — oil and gas, defense contracting, real estate. A forensic accountant who audited the opposing party's books three years ago is a problem that surfaces at the worst possible moment.
- **Ask about report turnaround explicitly.** Most engagements run 60–120 days for a full expert report, but complex fraud reconstructions can stretch longer. Get a timeline commitment in writing before engagement.

> **Pro Tip:** If you're in Tarrant County family court dealing with a high-asset divorce, prioritize experts with specific experience in closely-held business valuation and lifestyle analysis. General litigation support specialists often underestimate the complexity of community property disputes involving operating businesses.

## What to Expect

Forensic accounting engagements in Fort Worth typically run $5,000 on the low end (a focused document review for a smaller claim) up to $75,000 or more for complex multi-year fraud reconstructions or high-stakes commercial litigation where the expert will testify. Hourly rates for credentialed forensic accountants in the DFW market generally fall between $250–$450/hour, with senior partners at larger firms billing higher.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake attorneys make is hiring based on the lowest hourly rate. A less experienced examiner who takes twice as long — and produces a report opposing counsel tears apart in Daubert — costs far more than the rate differential. The deliverable is a defensible expert opinion, not billable hours.

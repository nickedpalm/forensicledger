The Detroit metro's legal market is dense with commercial litigation, automotive industry disputes, and a steady pipeline of fraud cases flowing through the Eastern District of Michigan federal court, yet credentialed forensic accountants who can actually hold up under cross-examination are harder to find than their sheer numbers suggest. This directory cuts through the noise.

## How to Choose a Forensic Accountant in Detroit

- **Verify the right credential for your case type.** A CFF (Certified in Financial Forensics) is the AICPA's forensic-specific designation — ideal for damages quantification and fraud. A CFE (Certified Fraud Examiner) is stronger for embezzlement and asset misappropriation. If your case involves business valuation, a CVA or ABV credential matters more than either. Don't let a generalist CPA talk you into a role the certification doesn't support.
- **Ask about Michigan-specific industry experience.** Detroit's economy means a disproportionate share of forensic engagements involve automotive suppliers, manufacturing contracts, and dealer franchise disputes. An accountant who's worked these sectors understands the revenue recognition quirks and intercompany transfer pricing issues that actually show up in the damages calculations.
- **Confirm they've testified in Michigan state and federal court.** The Eastern District of Michigan and Wayne County Circuit Court have their own rhythms. An expert who's been qualified and survived Daubert challenges in both venues is categorically different from one who's only written reports.
- **Check for conflicts before you brief them.** Detroit's forensic accounting community is smaller than it looks. Major firms cross-pollinate with the Big Four and regional practices, and an expert retained by opposing counsel last year is a problem you don't want to discover during voir dire.
- **Get the retainer structure in writing before the first call.** Hourly billing without a project cap is a common trap. Ask upfront whether the engagement includes report drafting, deposition prep, and trial testimony — or whether those are billed separately.

> **Pro Tip:** Run the expert's name through PACER before you hire. If they've been excluded under Daubert or had an opinion struck in a prior case, you need to know that before opposing counsel does.

## What to Expect

Forensic accounting engagements in Detroit typically run $5,000–$75,000 depending on case complexity — a straightforward insurance claim sits at the low end, while a multi-year commercial fraud reconstruction or contested business valuation in a high-stakes divorce can blow past $75,000 before trial. Most engagements take 6–12 weeks from intake to expert report, though expedited timelines are negotiable at a premium.

> **Reality Check:** The most common pricing mistake attorneys make is hiring based on hourly rate rather than total engagement cost. A $350/hour expert who scopes tightly and writes a clean report is cheaper than a $250/hour expert who needs three rounds of revisions and a supplemental analysis because the initial scope was vague. Get a written scope of work, not just a rate card.

## Local Market Overview

Detroit's forensic accounting demand is driven by three overlapping markets: the Eastern District of Michigan's active commercial docket (which has seen a sustained run of automotive supplier disputes and franchise termination cases), the Wayne County Probate and Circuit courts handling complex estate and divorce valuations, and the insurance industry's regional claims operations concentrated in Southfield and Troy. That combination — manufacturing litigation, high-asset family law, and insurance fraud — means the best Detroit-area forensic accountants tend to be narrow specialists rather than generalists, and matching the expert's depth to your case type is the single biggest variable in engagement quality.

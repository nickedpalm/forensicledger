## How to Choose a Forensic Accountant in New Orleans

- **Lead with credentials, not just "CPA."** A CPA license is table stakes. For litigation support, you want CFF (Certified in Financial Forensics), CFE (Certified Fraud Examiner), or ABV (Accredited in Business Valuation) — these indicate someone who has specifically trained for adversarial financial analysis, not just tax prep.
- **Ask about Louisiana-specific case experience.** New Orleans courts see a heavy volume of insurance disputes, hurricane business interruption claims, and maritime fraud cases. A forensic accountant who has worked BP/Deepwater Horizon-era claims or energy sector disputes has a materially different skill set than one who hasn't.
- **Verify expert witness history before you engage.** Request a CV with prior testimony — federal and state courts, number of cases, which side retained them. If they can't produce a list, keep moving.
- **Match the engagement type to the specialist.** Fraud investigation (think embezzlement, Ponzi schemes) and business valuation in divorce proceedings require different methodologies. Some firms do both well; many don't. Be explicit about your case type upfront.
- **Check for conflicts early.** New Orleans's professional community is tight. The forensic accountant you want may have worked with the opposing firm, a key witness, or the subject company. A five-minute conflict check saves you three weeks of discovery headaches.

> **Pro Tip:** If opposing counsel has already retained someone, look at who that expert has *not* worked with in the past five years. In a market this size, the pool of truly independent experts is smaller than it looks.

## What to Expect

Expect to spend $5,000 on the low end for a focused document review or a narrow damages rebuttal, and $75,000 or more for a complex commercial dispute involving reconstructed transactions, multiple entity structures, and full expert report plus trial testimony. Retainers are standard — most experienced forensic accountants won't open a file without one. Timeline from engagement to a draft expert report typically runs six to twelve weeks depending on document volume.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake attorneys make is engaging a forensic accountant too late, then paying rush premiums. Bringing someone in during discovery — before depositions — costs the same hourly rate and produces dramatically better results. Last-minute engagements also signal weakness to opposing counsel.

New Orleans sits at the intersection of maritime law, energy litigation, and a persistent insurance claims market still processing losses from major weather events — which means forensic accountants here tend to have deep experience in exactly the high-stakes, document-intensive engagements that require both technical precision and credible courtroom presence. The Eastern District of Louisiana is one of the busier federal dockets in the South, and the local bar expects experts who can hold up under the kind of aggressive cross-examination that complex commercial cases generate.

## How to Choose a Forensic Accountant in Albuquerque

- **Verify the credential stack, not just "CPA."** A CPA license is table stakes. Look for a CFF (Certified in Financial Forensics) or CFE (Certified Fraud Examiner) for litigation and fraud work, or a CVA/ABV if business valuation is central to your damages theory. New Mexico has a small but active ACFE chapter — membership is a reasonable signal of ongoing engagement with the field.
- **Ask for New Mexico district court experience specifically.** Federal court in the District of New Mexico (Albuquerque division) has its own rhythm. An expert who's been qualified and cross-examined in Bernalillo County District Court is a different animal than someone who's only written reports.
- **Match the expert to the case type.** Commercial fraud cases and divorce/marital dissolution cases require different instincts. Don't hire a business interruption specialist for a community property dispute — and vice versa.
- **Request a sample redacted report.** Any credentialed expert should be able to produce one. You're looking at organization, citation discipline, and whether their methodology would survive a Daubert challenge.
- **Check for prior adverse rulings.** Ask directly. A good expert will tell you about cases where their testimony was excluded and explain why. An evasive answer is its own data point.

> **Pro Tip:** New Mexico's oil and gas economy generates a specific class of commercial disputes — partnership dissolutions, royalty fraud, and business valuation fights tied to energy assets. If your matter touches that sector, prioritize experts with hydrocarbon-industry accounting experience. Several Albuquerque firms have it; most don't advertise it prominently.

## What to Expect

Forensic accounting engagements in Albuquerque typically run $5,000–$75,000 depending on complexity — a straightforward embezzlement reconstruction sits at the low end, while a multi-year commercial damages analysis with deposition and trial testimony can hit the ceiling fast. Most experts bill hourly ($250–$500/hr for credentialed specialists), with document review, report preparation, and testimony billed separately. Expect 4–8 weeks for a standard expert report; rush timelines are possible but command a premium.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake attorneys make is scoping only the report and forgetting about deposition prep, rebuttal work, and trial testimony. Get a full engagement estimate — including anticipated hours for each phase — before you sign. A $15,000 report can become a $45,000 engagement if opposing counsel is aggressive.

The Bernalillo County courthouse handles a high volume of domestic relations cases with significant asset complexity, making matrimonial forensic work one of the steadier practice areas in the market.

## How to Choose a Forensic Accountant in Denver

- **Match credentials to case type.** A CFF (Certified in Financial Forensics) is your default for litigation support and damages work. Bring in a CFE (Certified Fraud Examiner) if the matter involves embezzlement or asset misappropriation. For business valuation disputes — divorce, shareholder oppression, M&A gone wrong — prioritize ABV or CVA holders.
- **Ask for Colorado courtroom experience specifically.** Denver's legal market runs through Denver District Court and the U.S. District Court for Colorado, and judges here have seen a lot of expert testimony. An accountant who's testified in front of Judge [Krieger or Arguello] has a different readiness level than one who's only written reports.
- **Verify they can handle Colorado's energy and real estate exposure.** Denver's economy concentrates risk in O&G royalty disputes, construction defect claims, and real estate partnership blow-ups. If your matter touches any of those, you want someone who's worked these industries before — the financial statements look different and the damages methodologies aren't generic.
- **Get a writing sample before you retain.** Expert reports are the work product. Ask for a redacted exemplar report. A forensic accountant who can't write clearly won't survive cross-examination on their own conclusions.
- **Confirm availability for your trial window.** Denver firms stay busy with [Colorado Springs](/locations/colorado/colorado-springs/) overflow and the federal court docket. If trial is in six months, ask where this engagement falls in their queue now — not after you've signed the engagement letter.

> **Pro Tip:** Colorado requires CPAs to maintain licensure through the Colorado State Board of Accountancy. Before you retain anyone, run their CPA number at dora.colorado.gov. Takes 30 seconds. Expired or disciplinary history is a gift to opposing counsel.

## What to Expect

Engagements in Denver typically run $5,000–$15,000 for a focused damages analysis on a straightforward commercial dispute; complex fraud reconstructions or multi-party litigation can push $50,000–$75,000 once you're into deposition prep and trial testimony. Most forensic accountants bill hourly ($300–$500/hr for credentialed principals) and require a retainer before review begins. Turnaround for a preliminary damages estimate is usually 2–4 weeks; a full expert report ready for disclosure runs 6–10 weeks depending on document volume.

> **Reality Check:** The most common pricing mistake attorneys make is scoping for "a quick review" and getting billed for a full expert engagement. Be explicit upfront about whether you need a consulting expert (confidential, no report) or a testifying expert (disclosed, reportable). The process — and the cost — diverges sharply at that fork.

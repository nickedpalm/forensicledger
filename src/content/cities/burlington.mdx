## How to Choose a Forensic Accountant in Burlington

- **Match credentials to the case type.** A CFE (Certified Fraud Examiner) is built for embezzlement and fraud investigations. A CFF or ABV is stronger for business valuation disputes and commercial litigation damages. Don't hire a fraud specialist for a business interruption claim and wonder why the report feels thin.
- **Ask for Vermont-specific testimony experience.** Vermont Superior Court and federal cases in the District of Vermont have their own procedural rhythms. An expert who's testified in Burlington or Montpelier courts will be more credible and less likely to get rattled under cross-examination by opposing counsel who knows the local bench.
- **Request a writing sample, not just a CV.** Expert reports live or die on clarity. Ask for a redacted prior report. If it's dense with unexplained jargon, that's what the jury gets too.
- **Verify their independence.** A forensic accountant who also does the company's annual audit is a conflict waiting to be exploited. In smaller Vermont markets, these overlaps happen more than they should.
- **Confirm availability before you commit.** Burlington's forensic accounting pool is thin. If you're on a hard litigation calendar, a specialist with a full docket will cost you your trial date.

> **Pro Tip:** Vermont has no state-level CFF or CFE licensing registry, so credential verification falls entirely on you. Confirm active membership directly with AICPA (for CFF/ABV) or ACFE (for CFE) before signing an engagement letter.

## What to Expect

Forensic accounting engagements in Burlington run $5,000 on the low end (limited document review, single-issue analysis) to $75,000 or more for complex commercial disputes with multi-year financial reconstruction, deposition prep, and live testimony. Retainers are standard — expect to fund $10,000–$20,000 upfront for mid-complexity cases. Turnaround on a preliminary damages opinion is typically four to eight weeks depending on document volume; trial-ready expert reports take longer.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake attorneys make here is hiring on hourly rate alone. A $200/hour generalist CPA who needs 400 hours to get up to speed on forensic methodology will cost you more — and deliver a weaker report — than a $400/hour CFF who's done this fifty times. The credential premium pays for itself in report quality and cross-examination resilience.
